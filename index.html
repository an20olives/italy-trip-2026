Knees and shoulders must be covered to enter any church. Strictly enforced at St. Peter's Basilica — no shorts or tank tops. A lightweight scarf in your bag solves the problem easily.

Footwear — Very Important

This tour is Level 3 of 4 for activity. Expect 2–3 miles of walking daily on cobblestones and uneven terrain. Wear comfortable, well broken-in shoes. Do not bring new ones.

👜

Luggage Allowance (Tauck)

One checked bag per person  ·  Max 50 lbs (23 kg)  ·  Max 62 inches total dimensions. Also verify your airline's own baggage policy separately.

💶

Currency

Italy uses the Euro (€). Check current rates at xe.com. Cards are widely accepted — carry some cash for small purchases and markets.

🕐

Time Zone

Italy is 6 hours ahead of Eastern Time in summer (UTC+2, CEST). If it's 9:00 AM in New York, it's 3:00 PM in Rome.

🌡️

Weather — Late June & Early July

Hot and sunny. Daily highs average 78–85°F and often exceed 90°F. Pack light breathable clothing, a hat, sunscreen, and a small umbrella. Check weather.com before departure.

🔌

Electrical Adapters

Italy uses 240V power with Type F/L outlets. Bring a universal travel adapter. Any personal hair dryer must be dual-voltage (100–240V).

🌐

Hotel Wi-Fi

Basic complimentary Wi-Fi available at all three hotels. Speed and reliability may vary by property.

🚨 Emergency Guide

Keep this page bookmarked. In any emergency, stay calm, stay together, and follow these steps.

🆘

Italian Emergency Numbers

112 — Single European Emergency Number (Police, Fire, Ambulance — English operators available)
113 — Italian State Police (Polizia di Stato)
118 — Medical Emergency & Ambulance
115 — Fire Brigade (Vigili del Fuoco)
1515 — Environmental Emergency

Tip: 112 is the most reliable number to call — it routes to the correct service and English-speaking operators are available 24 hours.

🕵️

If You Are Pickpocketed — Step by Step

Pickpocketing is the most common crime targeting tourists in Rome, Florence, and Venice. High-risk areas include the Colosseum, Vatican, train stations, crowded markets, and public transport. If it happens, here is exactly what to do:

Step 1 — Stay calm and stay together. Do not chase the pickpocket. It is not worth the risk of confrontation or becoming separated from the group in an unfamiliar area.

Step 2 — Check what was taken. Identify exactly what is missing — phone, wallet, passport, or cards — so you can report accurately.

Step 3 — Call your Tauck Director immediately. They have handled this before and will guide you through the local process. Tauck 24-hour line: 1-800-468-2825.

Step 4 — Cancel your cards right away. Call your bank or use your banking app the moment you realize a card is missing. Do not wait until after the police report.

Step 5 — File a police report (Denuncia). Go to the nearest Polizia di Stato station and file a formal written report — this is called a denuncia. You will need this document to make any insurance claim, replace a passport, or dispute fraudulent card charges. Ask your Tauck Director or hotel concierge to assist with translation if needed.

Step 6 — If your passport was stolen, contact the nearest U.S. Embassy or Consulate (numbers above) to begin emergency passport replacement. Bring your police report, a photocopy of your passport photo page (which you should have kept separate), and a passport-style photo if possible.

Step 7 — File a travel insurance claim. Contact CareFree Travel Assistance at 1-800-208-6173 and file a claim at aontravelclaim.com. Keep all receipts and the police report number.

🛡️

Pickpocket Prevention Tips

Wallets & phones: Keep them in a front pocket or a zipped interior bag pocket — never a back pocket or an open bag.

Bags: Wear crossbody bags in front of your body in crowded areas. Do not hang a bag on the back of your chair at a restaurant.

Passports: Leave your actual passport in the hotel safe. Carry a photocopy — it is accepted at most museums and sites and cannot be stolen for travel fraud.

Distractions: Be alert if someone approaches you with a petition, drops something near you, or causes a commotion — these are common distraction techniques used by pickpocket teams.

Cards: Only carry the cards you need for the day. Leave extras locked at the hotel. Consider carrying a small amount of emergency cash separately from your main wallet.

Crowds: Extra vigilance on public transport, escalators, and at popular monuments — these are the highest-risk moments. Keep hands on your belongings when moving through tight spaces.

🏥

If Someone Has a Medical Emergency

Step 1 — Call 118 (Italian ambulance) or 112 immediately for any serious medical emergency. State your location as clearly as possible — use the hotel address or the nearest landmark.

Step 2 — Contact your Tauck Director. They will coordinate with local health authorities and notify Tauck's support team on your behalf.

Step 3 — Contact CareFree Travel Assistance. US: 1-800-208-6173  ·  International collect: +1-817-826-7005. They coordinate medical consultations, local hospital referrals, and emergency evacuation if required.

Step 4 — Document everything. Request a written report from the treating physician and keep all receipts. These are required to file your travel insurance claim with Aon at aontravelclaim.com.

Note: Italian public hospitals (Ospedale) are required to treat emergency patients regardless of insurance status. Your travel insurance covers up to $100,000 in medical expenses and up to $500,000 for emergency evacuation.
